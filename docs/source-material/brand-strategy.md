# Jukkai by Crystelle Terrasson

## Brand Strategy

March 2026 — Updated June 2026

---

## Who is Crystelle Terrasson

Crystelle Terrasson is an interior architect. Not a decorator, not a stylist. An architect of interior spaces, UNAID-qualified, with 14 years of practice across residential and professional projects. She handles full missions: conception, technical planning, contractor management, site supervision.

Her clients consistently underestimate the technical depth of what she does. They come in thinking about colours and finishes. They discover someone who checks structural norms, verifies material codes, manages artisan teams, and carries legal responsibility for every choice. The surprise is always the same: they didn't know how serious this work is.

What makes her different is where she goes once the serious work is handled. Every project comes with constraints. Budget, structure, regulations, client habits. Crystelle treats constraints as creative territory. In her words: "Chaque contrainte est un terrain de jeu." The client gets something technically sound that also makes them say "on n'aurait jamais fait ça." That reaction is what she works toward. Not applause, not gratitude. The moment when someone sees their own space and doesn't recognise what they thought was possible.

She has run Studio Crystelle Terrasson for 14 years. Leads come almost entirely through referral and word of mouth. Her name carries real weight in the territory. That name is now the foundation of something new.

## What is Jukkai

Jukkai is an art shop. Not a gallery.

The distinction matters. A gallery implies white walls, conceptual work, hushed reverence, high prices, and a door most people feel they need permission to walk through. Jukkai is the opposite. You push the door open and you're in a colourful, busy, alive interior. Art on every wall. Sculptures on columns, large and small. Iconic furniture pieces. Bold wallpapers, painted surfaces. Things at every price point. You want to stay, to wander, to look at things. Someone offers you a coffee.

The art is primarily urban, pop art, contemporary. Colourful above all. The price range runs from around 100€ for an accessible small format to 2,000–3,000€ for more ambitious pieces. The heart of the offer sits at 300–400€. Enough to feel like a real acquisition, not so much that it intimidates a first-time buyer. The core promise is simple: buying art should feel natural, not like an event.

Jukkai is scheduled to open around October 1, 2026 in Châteaugiron, a Petite Cité de Caractère in Ille-et-Vilaine. The space is not just a shopfront. Behind vitrines, visitors can see the architecture studio at work. Screens display interior design projects. A meeting room doubles as a welcoming space. The art and the architecture practice share the same physical footprint, deliberately. You cannot visit one without encountering the other.

The name is Japanese: 述懐 (jukkai) — the gesture of pouring out one's deepest feelings. Literally: 述 (narrate) + 懐 (the heart's return). It names the moment when everything carried inside finally finds expression — which is what both the studio and the art shop do: give form to what someone feels but can't yet articulate. The double "k" gives the word physical commitment; the soft French "j" and the open "ai" ending make it land warm and bright. For those who ask, there's a story. For those who don't, it sounds intentional with a world behind it.

### What Jukkai is not

This list comes directly from Crystelle and is non-negotiable for every future brand decision:

Not a boutique déco. Not a distribution store. Not a place that sells "objects." Not pure concept art. Not all-white, not cold, not minimal. Not expensive-feeling. Not intimidating. Not a gallery in the traditional sense. Not a place where you whisper.

Alive. Always alive.

### What is real for opening

The physical space, the art shop, the studio behind the vitrines, the screens, the meeting room. Artist sourcing is underway with contacts advancing: Raphaël Emmery (pop art illustration), Yohan Bonneville (contemporary, graffiti-influenced). The inventory will be curated by Crystelle personally.

Vernissages, live-painting events, and a regular exhibition programme are ambitions for after the first three months. They are not part of the launch scope.

## The Fusion

This is the strategic core of the entire project.

Crystelle is an interior architect who is opening an art shop. Those two activities share a physical space, a name, a philosophy, and a client ecosystem. The positioning is not "an interior architect who also sells art" and it is not "an art shop run by someone who happens to be an architect." It is one integrated world where art and interior architecture are two expressions of the same eye.

This fusion is Jukkai's only real differentiator. There are other galleries in Châteaugiron. There are other interior architects in the region. Nobody is both, and nobody is making the connection between the two a deliberate part of their offer. The closest local competitor is a contemporary art gallery doing conceptual work in a traditional gallery format. Crystelle is doing something structurally different.

The bridge works in both directions. A studio client discovers an artwork through a residential project and goes to the artist. A Jukkai visitor sees the architecture work on screens, enters a conversation, and eventually becomes a studio client. Crystelle has already placed artworks in interior projects, though it has only happened a few times so far. The bridge exists in practice but is not yet a habit.

One important observation: Crystelle naturally leads with the art shop when describing Jukkai. The architecture studio is established, familiar, second nature. The art shop is the new energy, the personal dream. This is completely natural at this stage. But it means the brand, the website, the visual identity, and the physical space need to carry the fusion visibly and structurally. The connection between art and architecture should feel obvious to every visitor without Crystelle having to explain it. The environment tells the story.

## The Audiences

### Private clients (interior architecture)

**Isabelle.** 40 years old. Buying an unrenovated house from the 1980s. Everything needs to be redone. Budget around 150,000€. She hands over the full mission because she doesn't have the time, the knowledge, or the confidence to manage contractors herself. She needs someone who takes the weight off her shoulders and delivers a result she couldn't have imagined on her own.

**Bernard.** 60 years old. Mortgage is paid off, children have left. He wants to modernise the house for the next chapter: a proper master bedroom, a bigger living room, a new kitchen. Budget around 100,000€. Mostly interior work, less structural. He has time and opinions but needs someone who translates those opinions into something that actually works.

**The young couple.** First-time buyers. Looking for advice more than a full mission. Budget-conscious. They need guidance within economic constraints.

**The déco client.** Focused project: refresh the living room, redo the kitchen. Budget around 40,000–50,000€. Not a full renovation, more of a targeted transformation.

What these clients share: they come through referral, they're trusting someone with their home, and the decision is emotional before it's rational. Can I trust her eye? Will she understand how I live? Will the result feel like me but better?

### B2B clients (interior architecture)

Companies renovating or creating office spaces. Missions tend to focus on conception and material specification rather than full site supervision. The relationship is more rational: credentials, process, references, capacity. They need to know she can handle the scale and deliver on time.

### Jukkai visitors (art buyers)

Not yet fully defined. Crystelle's intuition: younger clients drawn by the energy and accessibility, and older clients with more disposable budget. B2B clients are not expected to cross over into art buying. The profile will become clearer once the space is open and real foot traffic reveals who actually walks in.

What is clear: the Jukkai visitor is not an art collector. They are someone who sees something they like and wants it in their home. The buying decision is aesthetic and emotional, not investment-driven. They don't want to be educated about art history. They want to feel something and take it home.

### Who Jukkai is not for

People who negotiate prices without understanding that an artist is behind the work. The art has value. The price reflects that. Jukkai is accessible, not cheap.

## The Economic Model

The studio sustains the overall business. This is established, profitable, and referral-driven. Jukkai does not need to be independently profitable to justify its existence.

Jukkai's economic role is threefold. First, it is a personal ambition. Crystelle wants to work with artists, live surrounded by art, curate a space that reflects her taste. This is a genuine motivation and it matters. Second, it is a positioning engine. Jukkai elevates the studio's visibility and distinctiveness. Even if no art sells in a given month, the gallery's existence makes the studio more interesting, more visible, and more memorable. Third, it is a potential revenue stream. Art sales at a 300–400€ sweet spot, with range from 100€ to 3,000€. Revenue is welcome but not the primary measure of success.

Crystelle's honest assessment: if Jukkai doesn't sell art for three months but generates architecture leads, that's acceptable. It would be a personal disappointment, not a business crisis. The studio carries the financial weight. Jukkai carries the brand.

At three years, if growth demands it, the two activities may need separate staffing. Crystelle acknowledges she cannot run both at scale alone. A dedicated person for the art shop is a likely future need.

## Brand Philosophy

**Chaque contrainte est un terrain de jeu.**

Every project, every space, every brief comes with limits. Budget, structure, taste, regulation, fear. Crystelle's work begins where most people see a wall. She finds the opening. The client gets something they didn't know they wanted. The artist gets a context they didn't expect. The visitor gets a space that feels impossible and inevitable at the same time.

This philosophy applies to both entities. The studio takes a boring brief and makes it surprising. Jukkai takes intimidating art and makes it land in your living room. Same instinct, two expressions.

## Tone of Voice

Jukkai's voice is:

**Warm but not soft.** There is real conviction here. Crystelle believes in what she does and it shows. The tone is welcoming, generous, open. But it is not tentative, not apologetic, not asking for permission. It says "come in" with confidence, not with a whisper.

**Colourful but not loud.** The brand has energy, personality, visual density. It is never beige, never safe, never playing it down. But it is also not shouting, not desperate for attention, not performative. It is someone at a party who is magnetic because they are genuinely interesting, not because they are the loudest.

**Accessible but not cheap.** The language is simple, direct, human. No art-world jargon, no architectural pretension, no marketing polish. But accessible does not mean disposable. There is craft here, expertise, years of practice. The tone respects the work and the people behind it.

**Expert but not academic.** Crystelle carries 14 years of technical depth. The brand can speak with authority when needed. But it never lectures, never condescends, never makes someone feel stupid for not knowing what UNAID means or what a CCTP is.

**Rock but not punk.** Crystelle described Jukkai as a person at a party: "très colorée, un peu rock, très souriante, un peu énigmatique." There is an edge. There is personality. There is something slightly unexpected. But it is controlled, intentional, curated. Not chaotic, not aggressive, not trying to shock.

## Visual Territory

The visual language must hold two things at once: the energy of the art shop and the credibility of the architecture practice. It should feel like walking into Jukkai. Busy, colourful, dense, but curated. A trained eye made this chaos belong together.

### What it feels like

Alive. Colourful. Confident in its own taste. Dense without being cluttered. A space where pop art lives next to architectural rigour and it feels natural. Typography that has presence. Imagery that has warmth. Layouts that have intention.

### What it never feels like

Cold. Minimal for the sake of minimal. All-white. Precious. Gallery-hushed. Generic "luxury interior design" aesthetic. Influencer-polished. Safe.

### References from Crystelle

**Kelly Arty** (Fontainebleau): the type of artists, the communication style, the regular events with artists. This is the community and programme energy Crystelle aspires to.

**Linea Design** (La Rochelle): the mix of objects, the boutique feeling. Pop, very design, numbered reproductions, accessible prices. This is the browsing and buying energy Crystelle wants in the physical space.

### Design direction (to be developed)

The territory sits between editorial confidence and physical warmth. Brutalist structure with organic curves. Bold typography with readable hierarchy. Colour as a language, not decoration. The website must work for a 58-year-old: legible, navigable, conventional in usability. The boldness is in the aesthetic, not in the interaction model.

Detailed visual direction will be developed through prototyping and moodboard sessions following this document.

## What Success Looks Like

**At one year (October 2027).** The artists who trusted Crystelle early don't regret it. They come back, they propose new collaborations. The studio has gained visibility through the art shop. Jukkai is recognised in Châteaugiron. People come in to buy gifts for Christmas. The space is alive and part of the town's rhythm.

**At three years.** Jukkai may need its own dedicated staff. The two activities might require separate organisation. The specifics are undefined, but the direction is clear: growth that demands more structure, not less.

**What Crystelle is not afraid of.** She was asked directly about her biggest fear. Her answer: "Si je me demande si je vais y arriver, je n'y vais pas. Donc pas de peur." This is not bravado. It is how she operates. She commits fully or not at all. The brand should carry that same conviction.

## Open Questions

These emerged from the discovery session and remain unresolved. They do not block the current phase but will need answers as the project develops.

**Jukkai's visitor profile.** Crystelle has intuitions but no data. The real audience will reveal itself once the space opens. The brand needs to be built for the intended audience while remaining flexible enough to adapt.

**Artist pipeline.** Sourcing is underway but not yet secured at scale. The launch inventory and the ongoing supply of new work are both open.

**Event programme.** Vernissages, live-painting, and cultural events are part of the vision but not confirmed for launch. The brand should not promise what doesn't yet exist.

**Three-year financial targets.** Crystelle cannot project revenue at this stage. The strategic framing (studio sustains, Jukkai elevates) holds regardless.

**Analytics and SEO setup.** jukkai.fr exists, but launch tracking still needs to be configured properly: GA4, Search Console, form submissions, click-to-call, and click-to-email. This is a launch requirement, not a brand-strategy blocker.
